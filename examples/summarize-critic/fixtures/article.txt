The Box That Shrank the World

On April 26, 1956, a converted wartime tanker called the Ideal X left Newark,
New Jersey for Houston carrying 58 aluminum boxes on its deck. The man behind
the voyage, trucking entrepreneur Malcom McLean, was not trying to invent a
technology. He was trying to avoid the cost of hand-loading cargo, where gangs
of longshoremen moved goods crate by crate, sack by sack, at a cost of roughly
$5.86 per ton. Loading the Ideal X cost about 16 cents per ton.

The box itself was the least interesting part. What mattered was everything
that had to change around it. Ships, trucks, railcars, cranes, and ports all
had to agree on the same dimensions and corner fittings before the container
could go anywhere. That agreement took more than a decade of fighting between
shipping lines, railroads, unions, and standards bodies, and was only settled
when the International Organization for Standardization published container
standards between 1968 and 1970.

Once the pieces snapped together, the economics were merciless. Port time fell
from weeks to hours. Freight costs fell so far that they nearly stopped
mattering to manufacturers, who discovered they could build factories wherever
labor was cheapest and ship components across oceans for pennies. Traditional
dockside districts in New York, London, and San Francisco emptied out, while
purpose-built container ports like Oakland, Felixstowe, and Singapore boomed.

The container did not just make shipping cheaper. It rearranged the world's
economic geography, and it did so almost invisibly, one identical steel box at
a time.
